For summer research at IIM Kozhikode I wanted to see how far you can push
**reinforcement learning on consumer hardware** — no A100s, just my laptop's
MX250. The testbed: [Kuhn Poker](https://github.com/Magniquick/gym-kuhn-poker),
a toy imperfect-information game that's small enough to reason about but still
requires mixed strategies.

## why not just CFR?

Counterfactual Regret Minimization is the standard answer for imperfect-info
games, and it's provably convergent. But it's also game-tree-shaped: you carry
regret tables over information sets. I wanted to know whether a plain
**Double DQN** — value-based, model-free — could recover something Nash-like
without any of that machinery.

## the result

- A working Gymnasium environment for Kuhn Poker (the first I could find).
- Double DQN converges to Nash-like strategies in **1–2 hours** on the MX250.
- The whole forward pass fits in **~3 MB**.

> The point isn't that DQN beats CFR — it doesn't, in general. The point is how
> *little* compute you need to get respectable play on a small game.

Paper and code are linked from the [research repo](https://github.com/Magniquick/reinforcement-learning).
